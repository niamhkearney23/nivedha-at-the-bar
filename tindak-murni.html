Arbitration  ·  Judgment in Default  ·  Section 10 AA 2005

The Arbitration Clause That Survived a Judgment in Default

11 April 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

When a contract contains a mandatory arbitration clause, can a creditor sidestep it by issuing a writ and taking a Judgment in Default when the other side doesn't enter appearance? The Court of Appeal settled this in Tindak Murni Sdn Bhd v Juang Setia Sdn Bhd [2020] 4 CLJ 301: the arbitration clause survives, and the JID does not.

The Facts

A dispute arose under a construction contract that contained a mandatory arbitration clause. Rather than initiating arbitration, the claimant issued a writ. When the defendant failed to enter appearance, the claimant applied for — and obtained — a Judgment in Default.

The defendant applied to set aside the JID and stay the court proceedings in favour of arbitration under section 10 of the Arbitration Act 2005. The claimant resisted, arguing that the defendant's failure to appear amounted to a waiver of its right to arbitrate.

The Issues

Three questions arose for the Court of Appeal:

01

Does section 10 of the Arbitration Act 2005 operate as a mandatory stay, or does the court retain a residual discretion to refuse?

02

Does a party's failure to enter appearance in court proceedings amount to a waiver of its right to arbitrate?

03

What must an applicant establish to obtain a stay under section 10?

⁂

"Section 10 is unequivocal. Where parties have agreed to arbitrate, the court has no discretion to refuse a stay. The arbitration clause must be honoured."

Court of Appeal, [2020] 4 CLJ 301

The Findings

01  ·  Section 10 is mandatory, not discretionary

Section 10 of the Arbitration Act 2005 uses the word "shall" — the court must stay proceedings where a valid arbitration agreement exists and a party applies before taking any step in the action. There is no residual discretion to refuse a stay, unlike the older s34 of the repealed Arbitration Act 1952.

02  ·  Failing to enter appearance is not a waiver

A defendant who does not enter appearance in court proceedings does not thereby submit to the court's jurisdiction or waive its contractual right to arbitrate. The right to arbitrate is a creature of contract — it is not lost by procedural inaction in the very proceedings that should not have been commenced.

03  ·  The threshold for a stay applicant is low

The applicant need only establish three things: (i) there is a valid arbitration agreement; (ii) the dispute falls within its scope; and (iii) no step has been taken in the proceedings that would amount to submission to jurisdiction. On the facts, all three were satisfied.

04  ·  JID set aside; proceedings stayed

The JID was set aside and the court proceedings stayed pending arbitration. The claimant, having agreed to arbitrate, was held to its bargain — regardless of its having obtained a JID in the meantime.

Takeaways

—

Section 10 AA 2005 is mandatory. A party to an arbitration agreement can always enforce it — the court has no discretion to refuse.

—

Issuing a writ and taking a JID against a counterparty with an arbitration clause is a high-risk strategy. The JID will likely be set aside.

—

"Taking a step in proceedings" is the critical trap. Apply for a stay before filing a defence or doing anything that submits to jurisdiction.

—

Arbitration clauses are sticky. They survive procedural defaults, non-appearances, and even a JID. If you agreed to arbitrate, arbitrate.
