The Adjudicator's Boundary: Why Jurisdiction Cannot Be Rescued from Outside the Claim

25 April 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

An adjudicator's jurisdiction under CIPAA is not a general mandate to do justice — it is a specific authority defined by the Payment Claim and Payment Response. What happens when an adjudicator reaches beyond those documents to rescue a deficient claim? The High Court in Anas Construction Sdn Bhd v JKP Sdn Bhd [2021] 2 CLJ 665 held that such a rescue is itself ultra vires.

The Facts

Anas Construction submitted a Payment Claim under CIPAA. The Payment Claim contained deficiencies — certain heads of claim were not adequately particularised within the claim document itself. The adjudicator, seeking to resolve the dispute on its merits, drew on materials outside the Payment Claim and Payment Response to supplement and shore up the deficient portions.

JKP challenged the adjudication decision on the basis that the adjudicator had exceeded jurisdiction by going outside the four corners of the claim documents.

The Issues

Three questions arose:

01

Is an adjudicator's jurisdiction strictly bounded by the Payment Claim and Payment Response?

02

Can an adjudicator unilaterally supplement a deficient claim by reference to materials outside the claim documents?

03

What is the consequence where an adjudicator acts in excess of jurisdiction?

⁂

"The adjudicator is master of their own procedure — but only within the four walls of the dispute as framed by the parties. They cannot rebuild the claim from outside."

High Court, [2021] 2 CLJ 665

The Findings

01  ·  Jurisdiction is claim-based, not dispute-based

Under CIPAA, the adjudicator's authority is derived from and limited to what the claimant puts in its Payment Claim and what the respondent puts in its Payment Response. The adjudicator has no freestanding mandate to resolve whatever dispute might exist between the parties — only the dispute as those documents define it.

02  ·  No power to rescue a deficient claim

Where a Payment Claim is deficient, the adjudicator cannot fill the gap by importing materials from outside the claim documents. To do so is to exercise jurisdiction that was never conferred. The deficiency in the claim is the claimant's problem to address — not the adjudicator's to cure.

03  ·  Distinguished from procedural latitude

This does not mean adjudicators are procedurally straitjacketed. They retain wide latitude in how they conduct the proceedings — gathering evidence, requesting further submissions, directing the parties. The prohibition is specific: it goes to the substantive scope of the dispute, not the mechanics of the hearing.

04  ·  Jurisdictional excess vitiates the decision

An adjudication decision made in excess of jurisdiction is void in the affected part and potentially as a whole. The court set aside the decision to the extent it was premised on the improperly imported materials.

Takeaways

—

Draft your Payment Claim with care. What you leave out cannot be rescued by the adjudicator — and cannot be imported later.

—

Jurisdiction is a ceiling, not a floor. Adjudicators work within the scope of the claim and response; they cannot expand it on their own initiative.

—

A decision tainted by jurisdictional excess can be set aside under s15 CIPAA. If the adjudicator has gone outside the claim documents, challenge it.

—

Adjudicators retain wide procedural discretion. The prohibition is specifically against expanding the substantive scope of the dispute — not against how the hearing is run.
