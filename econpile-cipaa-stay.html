CIPAA  ·  Enforcement  ·  Section 16 Stay

The Point of No Return: Why You Cannot Stay an Adjudication Decision After Enforcement

18 April 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

CIPAA gives losing parties a window to apply for a stay of an adjudication decision pending final dispute resolution. But what happens when the winning party moves faster — obtaining an enforcement order before you apply for a stay? The Court of Appeal's answer in Econpile (M) Sdn Bhd v ASM Development (KL) Sdn Bhd [2021] 3 MLJ 157 is that you have crossed the point of no return.

The Facts

Econpile obtained an adjudication decision in its favour under CIPAA. Instead of applying for a stay under s16(1)(b) CIPAA to halt enforcement, ASM Development failed to act in time. Econpile moved quickly — it applied to the High Court to enforce the adjudication decision as a judgment under s28 CIPAA, and the enforcement order was granted.

Only after the enforcement order was already in place did ASM Development apply for a stay. The question before the court was whether that application was still available.

The Issues

Three questions fell for determination:

01

Does s16(1)(b) CIPAA permit a stay application after an enforcement order has already been made under s28?

02

Is there any other basis on which the court can stay enforcement of an adjudication decision that has been converted to a judgment?

03

What is the proper timing for a stay application under CIPAA's statutory scheme?

⁂

"Once the court has made an order enforcing the adjudication decision, the stay mechanism under section 16 is spent. The ship has sailed."

Court of Appeal, [2021] 3 MLJ 157

The Findings

01  ·  The stay window closes at enforcement

Section 16(1)(b) permits a stay of an adjudication decision pending arbitration or court proceedings on the merits. But this mechanism is designed to operate before enforcement — it cannot be used to undo an enforcement order that has already been made. The statute contemplates a sequential process, and ASM Development missed its place in the queue.

02  ·  Enforcement converts the decision to a court judgment

Once the High Court grants an order under s28, the adjudication decision takes effect as a judgment of the court. At that point, a party seeking to resist payment must attack the judgment itself — a significantly higher burden than a s16 stay application.

03  ·  CIPAA's "pay first" policy is upheld

The scheme of CIPAA is temporary finality: pay now, argue later. Permitting a stay after enforcement would unravel this policy and hand dilatory respondents a second bite at delay — the very mischief CIPAA was enacted to cure.

04  ·  No residual discretion imported

The court declined to recognise a general common law discretion to stay enforcement outside the statutory framework of s16. The legislature prescribed a mechanism and a timeframe; courts should not create a parallel route that bypasses both.

Takeaways

—

Timing is everything. Apply for a s16 stay before the other side races to enforcement — once an enforcement order is made, you are too late.

—

Once an enforcement order is granted under s28, a s16 stay application is spent and unavailable.

—

If you want to resist the adjudication decision itself, your route is a s15 set-aside application — not a stay of a judgment you allowed to be entered.

—

CIPAA's "pay now, argue later" philosophy is a feature. Courts will enforce it. Build urgency into your CIPAA response strategy from day one.
