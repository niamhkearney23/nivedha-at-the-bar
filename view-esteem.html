The Respondent's Second Chance: Raising New Defences in Adjudication

2 May 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

A respondent serves a Payment Response. Does that mean its defences in the Adjudication Response are frozen to what it said there — or can it raise new grounds? The Federal Court in View Esteem Sdn Bhd v Bina Puri Holdings Bhd [2019] 5 CLJ 479 gave respondents a significant reprieve: natural justice requires the door to remain open.

The Facts

Bina Puri Holdings served a Payment Claim on View Esteem. View Esteem served a Payment Response raising limited grounds of dispute. When the matter proceeded to adjudication, View Esteem sought to raise additional defences in its Adjudication Response — grounds that had not appeared in the Payment Response.

The adjudicator refused to consider these new defences on the basis that they were not raised in the Payment Response and were therefore inadmissible at the adjudication stage. View Esteem challenged the resulting decision on natural justice grounds.

The Issues

Three questions came before the Federal Court:

01

Is a respondent limited in its Adjudication Response to only those defences raised in its Payment Response?

02

Does preventing a respondent from raising new defences at the adjudication stage violate the rules of natural justice?

03

What is the proper scope of the Adjudication Response under CIPAA?

⁂

"To silence a respondent at the adjudication stage is to hollow out the process. CIPAA demands a fair hearing — not a one-sided one."

Federal Court, [2019] 5 CLJ 479

The Findings

01  ·  The Payment Response does not cap the Adjudication Response

CIPAA does not provide that a respondent is confined to defences raised in the Payment Response. The Payment Response and the Adjudication Response are separate documents serving separate functions. Nothing in the statute expressly limits the Adjudication Response to the grounds in the Payment Response.

02  ·  Natural justice demands a full hearing

The rules of natural justice — in particular audi alteram partem — require that a respondent be given a fair opportunity to answer the claim against it. Restricting the Adjudication Response to the Payment Response defences would deny this and reduce adjudication to a one-sided process, inconsistent with its quasi-judicial character.

03  ·  Section 27 CIPAA read consistently with natural justice

Section 27, which governs the adjudication proceedings, must be interpreted in a manner consistent with the rules of natural justice. An interpretation that silences the respondent on new defences cannot be correct and would create a constitutional question about the validity of the process itself.

04  ·  Decision set aside for breach of natural justice

By wrongly excluding the new defences, the adjudicator denied View Esteem a fair hearing. The adjudication decision was set aside. The adjudicator's procedural ruling had converted a statutory fast-track process into something fundamentally unfair.

Takeaways

—

Respondents are not locked in by the Payment Response. The Adjudication Response can — and should — raise all available defences.

—

But do not treat this as a reason to file a thin Payment Response. Raising defences early is still better practice — it signals credibility and forces the claimant to respond.

—

Natural justice is not a procedural nicety in CIPAA adjudications — it is a freestanding ground to set aside a decision under s15.

—

Adjudicators must consider all properly raised defences, including those not in the Payment Response. Shutting them out creates appellate exposure.
