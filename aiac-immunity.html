The AIAC's "Invisible Shield": Why Judicial Review Cannot Bypass Statutory Immunity

23 May 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

In statutory adjudication, the Asian International Arbitration Centre acts as the central administrative authority. When a party disputes an adjudicator's appointment or the AIAC's administrative fees, can it sidestep the adjudication process by commencing judicial review against the AIAC itself? The Federal Court answered this decisively in Asian International Arbitration Centre v One Amerin Residence Sdn Bhd & Ors [2025] 2 MLJ 634.

The Facts

The dispute arose from a payment claim between One Amerin Residence and its contractor, Ragawang Corp. After Ragawang initiated adjudication proceedings under CIPAA, One Amerin challenged three things: the validity of the adjudicator's appointment, the legality of the AIAC Rules, and the AIAC's authority to impose administrative fees.

One Amerin then commenced judicial review proceedings, seeking to quash the appointment and challenge the AIAC's administrative framework — effectively attempting to use the courts to derail the adjudication before it concluded.

The Issues

Three questions arose for the Federal Court:

01

Does the AIAC's statutory immunity extend to its administration of domestic adjudications under CIPAA, or only to its international arbitration functions?

02

Do immunity provisions covering "suit" and "other legal process" extend to judicial review proceedings?

03

What must a party show to displace the AIAC's immunity?

⁂

"The AIAC cannot be turned into a collateral litigation target to stall adjudication. Its immunity functions as a protective shield — not impenetrable, but formidable."

Federal Court, [2025] 2 MLJ 634

The Findings

01  ·  Functional but practically absolute immunity

The AIAC is a supranational organisation established under a host country agreement between Malaysia and AALCO. Under the International Organizations (Privileges and Immunities) Act 1992, it enjoys functional immunity which, within the scope of its functions, operates in substance as effectively absolute — subject only to bad faith or ultra vires conduct.

02  ·  No international/domestic distinction

The Court of Appeal had held that immunity applied only when the AIAC acted as an international arbitral institution, not when administering domestic adjudications under CIPAA. The Federal Court rejected this. The AIAC's mandate extends broadly across ADR functions, and CIPAA adjudication falls squarely within them.

03  ·  Judicial review falls within "suit" and "legal process"

The terms "suit" and "other legal process" must be interpreted broadly. Judicial review is included. A narrower reading would undermine the legislative intent of protecting the AIAC's independence and would create an obvious tactical loophole.

04  ·  Presumption of legality

Acts of an international organisation are cloaked with a presumption of legality. To overcome immunity, a party must show ultra vires conduct or bad faith — dishonesty, malice, or abuse of power. On the facts, the adjudicator was validly appointed, the AIAC Rules were lawfully made, and the administrative fees were validly imposed.

05  ·  Immunity clauses are not ouster clauses

The IOPIA and section 34(1) of CIPAA do not remove the High Court's jurisdiction. They shield the AIAC from proceedings where it acts in good faith within its functions. The distinction matters: jurisdiction is intact; the AIAC is simply protected from being made a defendant.

Takeaways

—

CIPAA's policy of fast, efficient adjudication is reinforced. The AIAC's role as gatekeeper is protected.

—

Judicial review cannot be used tactically to derail adjudication proceedings against the AIAC.

—

Immunity is not absolute. Bad faith or ultra vires conduct remains a viable route to challenge — but the threshold is high.

—

If dissatisfied with an adjudication outcome, the proper remedy is a section 15 CIPAA set-aside application — not proceedings against the AIAC itself.
