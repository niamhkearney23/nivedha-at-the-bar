CIPAA  ·  Jurisdiction  ·  Late Payment Interest

You Cannot Adjudicate Pure Interest: The Jurisdictional Limits of CIPAA Once the Principal Is Paid

27 June 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

In Brief

Section 4 of CIPAA defines “payment” as money for work done or services rendered. The Court of Appeal in Mayfair Ventures Sdn Bhd v Setiakon Builders Sdn Bhd [2025] MLJU 3699 held that late payment interest — even if contractually agreed — is a claim for damages, not a claim for payment. Once the principal sum has been settled, the adjudicator lacks jurisdiction to hear the remaining interest claim. Paying the principal before the Payment Claim is served effectively extinguishes the contractor’s right to adjudicate on those certificates.

The Facts

Setiakon (contractor) brought an adjudication claim for approximately RM8.8 million. The claim included unpaid balances on two certificates and late payment interest accumulated across forty-six progress certificates (Certificates 1 to 46). Mayfair (employer) had already settled the principal sums on Certificates 1 to 43 in full before the adjudication commenced. Setiakon nonetheless sought the interest that had accrued during those earlier delays under the PAM standard form.

The adjudicator allowed a significant portion of the interest claim. The High Court upheld the decision. The Court of Appeal reversed it, striking out the interest awarded on the already-settled certificates.

The Issues

Three questions fell to be determined:

01

Did the adjudicator have jurisdiction to hear a claim for late payment interest on certificates where the principal had already been paid in full before the Payment Claim was served?

02

Is late payment interest a “payment” under Section 4 of CIPAA, or is it a claim for damages for breach of contract?

03

Was Mayfair estopped from raising a jurisdictional challenge at the setting-aside stage, having failed to raise it in the Payment Response or Adjudication Response?

⁂

“Late payment interest is a claim for damages for breach of contract — not payment for work done or services rendered. Once the principal is paid, an adjudicator lacks jurisdiction to award what remains.”

Court of Appeal, Mayfair Ventures v Setiakon [2025] MLJU 3699

The Findings

01  ·  Jurisdiction vs. Power — a necessary distinction

The Court drew a clear line between jurisdiction (the adjudicator’s right to hear the matter, defined by the subject matter the statute permits) and power (the adjudicator’s tools to resolve a dispute once jurisdiction is established, including the power to award interest and costs). The two are not interchangeable. Broad adjudicatorial powers cannot rescue a claim that never belonged in adjudication to begin with. The subject matter must fall within the statute first; the powers operate only after that threshold is crossed.

02  ·  Interest is damages, not payment

Section 4 of CIPAA defines “payment” as money payable for work done or services rendered under a construction contract. The Court of Appeal held that late payment interest — even where contractually agreed under the PAM standard form — is a claim for damages for breach of contract. It is not payment for work. A standalone interest claim on certificates where the principal has already been settled therefore falls outside the scope of CIPAA entirely.

03  ·  The “unpaid party” requirement

CIPAA is a remedy for an “unpaid party.” Where Mayfair had settled the principal amounts for Certificates 1 to 43 before the Payment Claim was served, Setiakon was not an unpaid party in respect of those certificates at the relevant time. A Payment Claim for interest alone on settled debts was invalid. The status is assessed as at the date the Payment Claim is served — not as at some earlier point when the principal remained outstanding.

04  ·  No estoppel for absolute jurisdictional defects

Mayfair had not raised the jurisdictional point in its Payment Response or Adjudication Response. Setiakon argued this silence amounted to a waiver. The Court rejected this. An absolute lack of jurisdiction — the kind created by statute — cannot be cured by procedural omission or consent between the parties. Jurisdiction is a creature of statute; parties cannot confer it on an adjudicator by agreement. Mayfair was entitled to raise the challenge at the setting-aside stage, and it succeeded.

Takeaways

—

A CIPAA claim must be anchored to an unpaid principal sum. Interest on an outstanding principal is recoverable consequentially — but not as a standalone claim on certificates that have already been fully paid.

—

“Unpaid party” status is assessed at the time the Payment Claim is served. Paying the principal before the Payment Claim lands is an effective defence to any subsequent interest adjudication on those certificates.

—

Pure interest claims on settled certificates belong in civil litigation or arbitration, not statutory adjudication. CIPAA is not the right forum.

—

An employer facing a pure interest adjudication has a jurisdictional defence that can be raised at the setting-aside stage — regardless of earlier procedural failures in the adjudication itself.

—

The jurisdiction/power distinction is not academic. An adjudicator’s powers operate only within a valid adjudication. Those powers cannot be used to create jurisdiction over a claim the statute does not permit.
