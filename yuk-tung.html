Termination Justified: When LAD Follows the Contractor Out the Door

9 May 2026  ·  Saturday Soliloquies

This Issue

When a contractor consistently fails to proceed with the works regularly and diligently, the employer may terminate. But can the employer also recover full Liquidated and Ascertained Damages for the delay period — even after termination? The Court of Appeal in Yuk Tung Concrete Sdn Bhd v Daya CMT Sdn Bhd [2020] 1 LNS 1314 said yes, and restored an award of RM23.3 million.

The Facts

Daya CMT engaged Yuk Tung Concrete as contractor for a construction project. From the outset, Yuk Tung failed to mobilise adequate resources and consistently fell behind programme. Daya CMT issued multiple notices of default. Yuk Tung failed to remedy the position to a satisfactory standard.

Daya CMT terminated the contract and claimed full LAD for the period of delay attributable to Yuk Tung's defaults. At first instance, the LAD award was reduced by the trial court. Daya CMT appealed, seeking restoration of the full sum.

The Issues

Three questions arose for the Court of Appeal:

01

What is the standard for "failing to proceed regularly and diligently" as a ground of contractual termination?

02

Does an employer's election to terminate the contract extinguish or reduce its right to claim LAD for the delay period?

03

Was Daya CMT entitled to rely on the accumulation of defaults over the project — or only the final triggering default — when exercising termination?

⁂

"LAD is not a penalty for termination — it is compensation for delay. Once the contractor causes the delay, the employer does not forfeit that remedy by choosing to terminate."

Court of Appeal, [2020] 1 LNS 1314

The Findings

01  ·  Regular and diligent progress — an objective test

The court applied an objective standard: would a reasonable employer, assessing the actual rate of progress against the programme, have concluded that completion on time was unlikely without significant intervention? On the facts, the answer was plainly yes. Inadequate manpower, poor site management, and persistent failure to meet milestones all pointed to a contractor who was not proceeding regularly and diligently.

02  ·  Termination does not extinguish the LAD claim

The court rejected the argument that by electing to terminate, Daya CMT had taken an option that precluded it from claiming LAD. LAD is compensation for the delay caused by the contractor's breach — the employer's subsequent termination does not forgive the breach, waive the loss, or reduce the accrued entitlement to damages.

03  ·  Antecedent defaults support termination

An employer is entitled to rely on a pattern of defaults accumulated over the project period when exercising a right to terminate — it is not limited to pointing only to the most recent or most acute default. A contractor cannot escape termination by curing the last breach while the overall performance remains deficient.

04  ·  RM23.3 million LAD restored in full

The Court of Appeal found no justification in the evidence for the first instance reduction of the LAD award. The full RM23.3 million was restored. The reduction had been made without adequate legal or factual foundation.

Takeaways

—

A pattern of poor site management, even if no single incident would alone ground termination, can cumulatively justify it. Document every default notice and every missed milestone.

—

Termination and LAD are not mutually exclusive. Employers can and should claim both — termination does not waive the delay claim.

—

Contractors under default notices: act immediately and document your remediation efforts thoroughly. Partial improvement is not enough if overall progress remains deficient.

—

The RM23.3 million outcome is a stark reminder that LAD clauses in Malaysian construction contracts carry real, enforceable bite.
